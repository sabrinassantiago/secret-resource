Soft boundaries before you fire

Why Warnings Matter

Most client relationships do not need to end in a firing. Sometimes people cross boundaries because they do not know the boundaries exist.

These templates give clients a chance to fix the problem before you walk away. They set clear expectations, document the issue, and give you proof that you tried to make it work.

If they ignore the warning, you fire them with a clear conscience. If they fix the behavior, you saved the relationship. Either way, you win.

Use these before you go straight to the nuclear option. But if they ignore the boundary after you set it, do not give them a third chance. That is when you move to the Mains.

01

The First Warning

When they cross a boundary for the first time

Ingredients: One clear boundary, documented proof you tried, zero room for confusion
Allergens: May cause discomfort in clients who thought your boundaries were suggestions
Best served: Within 24 hours of the violation while it is still fresh
Chef's Note: This is the nice version. Direct but not aggressive. If they respect you, they will fix it. If they do not, you have proof you tried before you moved to the nuclear option. Most clients just need to be told the boundary exists. Some will test it anyway. Those are the ones you fire next month.
The Recipe
Hi [Client Name], I wanted to touch base about [specific issue: late payment / scope creep / communication boundary]. Our agreement was [state the original term]. Moving forward, I need us to stick to this so I can continue delivering quality work for you. Let me know if you have questions about how we can make this work. [Your Name]

Pairs with: Screenshotting the original agreement

02

The Scope Reset

When they keep asking for just one more thing

Ingredients: Original agreement, list of out-of-scope requests, polite offer to quote new work
Allergens: Contains boundaries. Not suitable for clients who think everything is included
Best served: After the third just one more thing request
Chef's Note: Scope creep is how good projects become bad projects. This stops the bleeding before you end up doing triple the work for the same money. Attach your original brief when you send this. Make them see in writing what they actually paid for. If they balk at paying for extra work, you just learned they were planning to exploit you the whole time.
The Recipe
Hi [Client Name], I want to make sure we are aligned on scope for this project. The original agreement covered [list deliverables]. The recent requests for [new items] fall outside this scope. I can absolutely help with these, but I will need to send you a separate quote. Would you like me to put together pricing for the additional work? [Your Name]

Pairs with: Your original contract attached to the email

03

The Communication Boundary

When they text you at 11pm or expect instant replies

Ingredients: Your actual working hours, response time expectations, emergency protocol
Allergens: Will disappoint clients who think freelancers never sleep
Best served: After the second time they message you at midnight
Chef's Note: This one tastes better the second time you enforce it. First time they will test you. Second time they will believe you. After you send this, stick to it. If they text at 10pm, do not reply until morning. Enforce the boundary or they will ignore it forever. Also stop feeling guilty about having a fucking life.
The Recipe
Hi [Client Name], I want to set clear expectations around communication so I can give you my best work. My working hours are [days and times]. I check email [frequency] and aim to respond within [timeframe]. For urgent issues, [specify emergency process]. This helps me stay focused on delivering quality results for you and my other clients. Thanks for understanding. [Your Name]

Pairs with: Turning off Slack notifications after 6pm
