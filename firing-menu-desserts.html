Desserts

The celebration

Firing a client feels terrible in the moment.

Your brain will try to convince you that you made a mistake: you should have tried harder, you are burning a bridge, bla, bla, bla. This bitch might even repeat that YOU are the one being difficult or unprofessional or mean.

Your brain is wrong.

Here is what is actually true.

What Really Happened

You just made space for better work
Every hour you spent managing a nightmare client was an hour you could not spend on clients who respect you, pay on time, and make your work better. You were trading your best hours for their worst behavior. Now you get those hours back.
You just set a boundary that will protect every future client relationship
The next time someone tries to pull the same behavior, you will spot it faster and shut it down sooner. You now know what your limit is and you are willing to enforce it. That makes every future relationship healthier.
You just proved to yourself that you can walk away
That means you are not desperate. You have standards and you are running a real biz, not just taking whatever comes your way. Clients can feel the difference between someone who needs them and someone who chooses them.
Bad clients do not get better
They get worse. You did not fix this by being patient or accommodating or giving them one more chance. You fixed it by ending it. The only way to solve a bad client problem is to fire the client.

The guilt will fade in a week.

The relief will last forever.

You might be worried about what they will say about you. Whether they will leave a bad review or badmouth you to other people or try to ruin your reputation.

Here is the truth about that too:

Some of them will.

And it might sting. A bad review, a vague warning passed to a mutual contact, a post in a community you care about. That is a real risk and pretending it isn't would be dishonest.

What you can actually do about it is:

Deliver a clean handover + be professional until the last email + keep records of everything.

That way, if anyone ever asks, your side of the story has receipts.

And most people, when they hear one person trashing a freelancer, will reserve judgment until they see the work for themselves. Your portfolio and good reviews speak louder than their complaint.

You cannot control what they say. You can only control how you leave.

Leave well.

Your reputation is not built on never saying no.

Your reputation is built on the quality of work you do for people who deserve it.

So what do you do now?

You close your laptop. Go for a walk... Call a friend... Make a delicious dinner (in my case, add guacamole to everything)... You do something that reminds you that you are a person with a life outside of this client relationship that just ended.

Tomorrow you send an email to your network letting them know you have availability. You reach out to past clients you loved working with and see if they have new projects coming up. Update your beautiful portfolio, darling, and do the work of filling the gap they left.

And in a week, maybe two, you will look back and wonder why you waited so long to fire them.

I'm so proud of you. And so should you.

You made the right call.

You did the right thing.
